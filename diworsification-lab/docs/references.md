# References

Curated literature behind the over-diversification thesis and the metrics used.

## Diminishing returns to diversification

- **Markowitz, H. (1952).** "Portfolio Selection." *Journal of Finance* 7(1),
  77–91. The origin of mean–variance portfolio theory and the formal role of
  covariance (not just variance) in portfolio risk.

- **Evans, J. L., & Archer, S. H. (1968).** "Diversification and the Reduction
  of Dispersion: An Empirical Analysis." *Journal of Finance* 23(5), 761–767.
  The classic empirical result that the marginal risk-reduction benefit of
  adding securities diminishes sharply beyond roughly 10–20 holdings.

- **Lynch, P. (1989).** *One Up on Wall Street.* Popularized the term
  **"diworsification"** — adding holdings that dilute returns without
  meaningfully reducing risk.

## Measuring true diversification

- **Choueifaty, Y., & Coignard, Y. (2008).** "Toward Maximum Diversification."
  *Journal of Portfolio Management* 35(1), 40–51. Introduces the
  **Diversification Ratio** and the most-diversified portfolio.

- **Meucci, A. (2009).** "Managing Diversification." *Risk*, pp. 74–79.
  Introduces the **Effective Number of Bets** via the entropy of the
  diversification distribution over principal components. SSRN:
  https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1358533

- **Meucci, A., Santangelo, A., & Deguest, R. (2013).** "Risk Budgeting and
  Diversification Based on Optimized Uncorrelated Factors." Introduces
  **minimum-torsion bets**, a refinement of PCA-based ENB tying uncorrelated
  factors to the investment process. SSRN:
  https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2276632

## Correlations in crises / stress regimes

- **Longin, F., & Solnik, B. (1995, 2001).** "Is the correlation in
  international equity returns constant: 1960–1990?" and "Extreme Correlation of
  International Equity Markets." Document that equity correlations rise in
  volatile/bear markets, especially in the lower tail.

- **Forbes, K., & Rigobon, R. (2002).** "No Contagion, Only Interdependence:
  Measuring Stock Market Comovements." *Journal of Finance* 57(5), 2223–2261.
  Shows that measured correlation rises mechanically with volatility
  (heteroskedasticity bias); essential caveat for any "correlations go to 1"
  claim.

- **Bekaert, G., Ehrmann, M., Fratzscher, M., & Mehl, A. (2014).** "Global
  Crises and Equity Market Contagion." *Journal of Finance*. NBER WP 17121.
  Factor-model evidence on transmission of the 2007–09 crisis.

- **Cizeau, P., Potters, M., & Bouchaud, J.-P. (2001).** "Correlation structure
  of extreme stock returns." *Quantitative Finance* 1(2), 217–222.

## Practitioner discussions of over-diversification

- CBRE Investment Management, "Hidden Risks of Overdiversification: When Less is
  More" — simulation evidence that 3–5 core funds capture most diversification
  benefit while limiting tracking error.
- Industry commentary consistently lands on ~5–7 well-chosen funds (or ~20–30
  uncorrelated stocks) as the point past which additional holdings mostly add
  cost and complexity rather than risk reduction.
